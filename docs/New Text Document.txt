It means this:

If you keep taking a chance with a **nonzero probability of success** (p>0), then the probability that you **eventually succeed at least once** approaches **100%** as the number of tries (n) becomes very large 📈

The formula is:

[
\lim_{n\to\infty} \left(1-(1-p)^n\right)=1
]

Here’s the intuition:

* (p) = chance of success on one try
* (1-p) = chance of failure on one try
* ((1-p)^n) = chance of failing **every single time** across (n) tries
* So,
  [
  1-(1-p)^n
  ]
  = chance of succeeding **at least once** in (n) tries

As (n) gets bigger and bigger, ((1-p)^n) gets closer to 0, so the whole expression gets closer to 1.

Example:

* suppose each try has a 10% success rate, so (p=0.1)
* failure each time = (0.9)
* fail 10 times in a row = (0.9^{10}\approx 0.35)
* so success at least once in 10 tries =
  [
  1-0.9^{10}\approx 0.65
  ]
  = 65%

If you try 100 times:
[
1-0.9^{100}\approx 0.99997
]

So basically: **keep playing long enough, and your chance of getting at least one win becomes extremely high**.

But — tiny math goblin warning 🧠⚠️ — this does **not** mean:

* you are guaranteed to win quickly
* every game is beatable by persistence
* the expected reward is always worth it
* this works if (p=0)

So the meme’s message is:
“Apply enough times, and statistically you’ll probably get one yes.”

That’s why it fits stuff like:

* job applications
* sales outreach
* startup pitching
* content posting
* networking

It’s basically probability theory saying: **volume matters when each attempt has a real chance**.

Not every factor should hit success probability in the same way.

There are **3 types of factors**:

## Type 1: Direct probability boosters

These directly improve chance of success on a given attempt.

Likely:

* skill (S)
* strategy (G)
* timing (T)
* environment (E)
* network (N)
* focus (U)

## Type 2: Growth / improvement factors

Likely:

* feedback (F)
* adaptability (A)

These should mostly change how skill or strategy evolves.

## Type 3: Persistence / survival factors

These affect whether you keep making good attempts.

Likely:

* consistency (C)

These affect actual number of attempts and whether effort continues after failure.

Absolutely — I went at this like we’re building a **real model**, not just a motivational poster with algebra 😄

After looking across meta-analyses, reviews, and theory papers, the clearest takeaway is this:

**Success is not best modeled as one flat multiplier.**
The evidence points to a **layered system**:

1. some factors change **whether you keep making attempts**
2. some change **how good each attempt is**
3. some change **how fast you improve over time**
4. some are **outside you**, like timing/opportunity conditions
5. several relationships are **nonlinear** and sometimes even **backfire** when pushed too far ([PubMed][1])

So the strongest research-backed structure is this:

[
P(\text{at least one success by } n)
====================================

1-\prod_{t=1}^{n}\left(1-a_t,p_t\right)
]

Where:

* (n) = planned attempts
* (a_t\in[0,1]) = probability the (t)-th planned attempt actually happens at usable quality
* (p_t\in[0,1]) = probability that the (t)-th actual attempt succeeds

That keeps your original probability logic intact, but now it lets all the other factors enter in the right places.

## 1) What the literature says each factor is really doing

**Skill/practice:** practice matters, but it does **not** explain most performance variance by itself. In one big meta-analysis, deliberate practice explained 26% of variance in games, 21% in music, 18% in sports, 4% in education, and less than 1% in professions. Also, learning usually shows **rapid early gains and later diminishing returns**, but individual learning curves vary a lot, so one universal curve shape is too simplistic. ([PubMed][1])

**Consistency:** the best-supported nearby construct is basically **perseverance/conscientiousness**, not some magical separate “hustle essence.” Grit is only moderately related to performance, overlaps heavily with conscientiousness, and its **perseverance** facet matters more than “consistency of interest.” Conscientiousness is repeatedly one of the strongest performance predictors, and in academics it adds prediction beyond intelligence. ([PubMed][2])

**Feedback:** feedback helps on average, but it is not automatically good. A classic meta-analysis found average improvement, but **more than one-third of feedback interventions actually reduced performance**. The big mechanism is attention: feedback works better when it keeps people focused on the **task and learning**, not ego threat or self-image. ([ResearchGate][3])

**Strategy:** clear strategy matters a lot. Specific difficult goals outperform vague goals, and business planning tends to help — but only in context. The best evidence is not “plan more forever”; it is “planning + learning” together. There is even evidence of an **inverted-U**: too little learning is bad, but too much can create overload and hurt the planning-performance link. ([PubMed][4])

**Timing:** opportunity conditions matter in ways that can dominate individual effort. Entering the labor market in a recession can affect earnings, wages, and employment outcomes far into life. Also, more applications do not always raise raw job-finding probability; they can instead improve **match quality**. So timing is not fluff — it changes the opportunity landscape itself. ([NBER][5])

**Environment/resources:** support structure matters because it affects engagement, bandwidth, and ability to execute. In longitudinal work, job resources predicted work engagement better than job demands, and job control was a particularly important predictor. ([ScienceDirect][6])

**Network/relationships:** the literature strongly suggests network effects are not just “how many people you know.” Career effects come through **information access, resource access, and sponsorship**, with network structure and social resources both mattering. Weak ties can be useful for new opportunities, but homophilous network search can also limit status attainment and reproduce inequality. ([ResearchGate][7])

**Emotional resilience:** resilience is best thought of as a **recovery and persistence capacity**, not a raw direct success booster. Emotion regulation tools like cognitive reappraisal are reliably linked with greater resilience, and the broader PsyCap literature treats resilience alongside hope, efficacy, and optimism as a performance-relevant resource bundle. ([ScienceDirect][8])

**Focus:** this one is brutally important. Task switching creates **attention residue**, and performance on the next task suffers when attention does not fully disengage from the previous one. Reviews of multitasking also find it is usually less efficient and can produce shallower learning. ([ScienceDirect][9])

**Adaptability:** adaptability is not just “being open-minded.” In the adaptive-performance literature, cognitive ability has a positive relationship with adaptation, around (r=0.21), stronger for **objective** adaptation measures and for highly dynamic tasks. But measurement is messy: subjective and objective adaptation do not fully agree. ([Paris-Lodron-University Salzburg][10])

## 2) The best model structure

The research points to three buckets.

### Bucket A: factors that affect whether attempts actually happen

These mostly govern (a_t):

* consistency (C_t)
* resilience (R_t)

So:

[
a_t = C_t \cdot R_t
]

Interpretation:

* (C_t): do you actually show up and execute as planned?
* (R_t): after setbacks, do you keep going at near-full strength?

## 3) Factors that affect the quality of each attempt

These mostly govern (p_t):

* base probability (p_0)
* skill (S_t)
* strategy (G_t)
* timing (T_t)
* environment (E_t)
* network (N_t)
* focus (U_t)

A clean form is logistic:

[
p_t
===

\sigma!\Big(
\operatorname{logit}(p_0)
+\beta_S S_t
+\beta_G G_t
+\beta_T T_t
+\beta_E E_t
+\beta_N N_t
+\beta_U U_t
+\beta_{SN}S_tN_t
+\beta_{GT}G_tT_t
\Big)
]

where (\sigma(x)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}).

Why this is a good choice:

* it keeps probability between 0 and 1
* gains can saturate
* interaction terms make sense:

  * **skill × network**: network opens doors better when you can convert them
  * **strategy × timing**: good strategy pays off more when opportunity timing is favorable

Those interaction choices are a synthesis from the literature, not something any one paper proved as a universal law. The evidence supports the idea that these factors combine rather than act in isolation. ([ResearchGate][7])

## 4) Factors that mostly drive improvement over time

These are the engines behind learning:

* feedback (F_t)
* adaptability (A_t)

So skill should **evolve**, not stay fixed:

[
S_{t+1}
=======

S_t
+
\eta_S , C_t U_t F_t A_t E_t ,(1-S_t)
-------------------------------------

\lambda_S (1-U_t)S_t
]

What this says:

* skill grows faster when you are consistent, focused, getting useful feedback, adapting well, and working in a supportive environment
* ((1-S_t)) creates diminishing returns
* low focus hurts by adding interference / switching cost, which matches the attention-residue and interference literature ([ScienceDirect][9])

Strategy can also improve over time:

[
G_{t+1}
=======

G_t
+
\eta_G F_t A_t (1-G_t)
]

That says better feedback + adaptability sharpen strategy over repeated attempts.

## 5) How to numerically define each factor

Now the fun part 🔥
Here’s a practical 0-to-1 version for every variable.

### Base chance

[
p_0 \in [0,1]
]

Empirical baseline in neutral conditions.
Example: if random cold outreach converts 2% of the time, start with (p_0=0.02).

### Attempts

[
n = \text{planned attempts}
]

### Skill

Best measured rather than guessed. But if you want a dynamic approximation:

[
S_t = 1-e^{-k_s X_t}
]

where (X_t) is accumulated high-quality practice.
This exponential saturation shape is safer than assuming a universal power law, because the literature shows strong heterogeneity across people and tasks. ([ScienceDirect][11])

### Consistency

Use both **adherence** and **regularity**:

[
C_t = A_t^{(cons)} \cdot Q_t^{(spacing)}
]

with

[
A_t^{(cons)}=\frac{\text{attempts completed}}{\text{attempts planned}}
]

and

[
Q_t^{(spacing)} = e^{-CV(\Delta t)}
]

where (CV(\Delta t)) is the coefficient of variation of spacing between attempts.
Reason: just cramming attempts is not the same as sustained regularity, and spaced practice often improves retention relative to massed practice. ([ScienceDirect][12])

### Feedback

[
F_t = Q_t^{(fb)} \cdot R_t^{(fb)} \cdot H_t^{(task)}
]

where:

* (Q_t^{(fb)}): feedback quality/specificity
* (R_t^{(fb)}): receptivity to feedback
* (H_t^{(task)}): how task-focused vs ego-focused the feedback is

That structure comes straight from the “feedback can help or harm depending on where attention goes” idea. ([ResearchGate][3])

### Strategy

[
G_t = \big(g_{\text{goal}} \cdot g_{\text{target fit}} \cdot g_{\text{plan}} \cdot g_{\text{learn-balance}}\big)^{1/4}
]

A good subformula for the planning-learning balance is an inverted-U:

[
g_{\text{learn-balance}} = 1-\gamma(L_t-L^*)^2
]

where (L^*) is the domain-specific sweet spot between rigid planning and flexible learning.
That mirrors the evidence that planning helps, learning helps, and too much unbounded learning can overload the system. ([ScienceDirect][13])

### Timing

[
T_t = \text{normalized opportunity index at time } t
]

For jobs, this could be built from labor-market tightness, vacancy rate, or hiring activity.
For startups, demand growth / funding climate.
For content, audience demand / distribution conditions.

### Environment

[
E_t = (e_{\text{control}} e_{\text{support}} e_{\text{tools}} e_{\text{time}} e_{\text{runway}})^{1/5}
]

I’d use a **geometric mean** here, because one missing pillar can choke execution.
That matches the resource-based logic better than a plain average. The research especially highlights job control/support as key resources. ([ScienceDirect][6])

### Network

[
N_t = (n_{\text{relevance}} n_{\text{trust}} n_{\text{reach}} n_{\text{activation}} n_{\text{sponsorship}})^{1/5}
]

Again, geometric mean is useful because:

* a huge network with no trust is weak
* trusted people with zero relevance are weak
* good contacts you never activate are weak

This lines up well with the literature showing that social capital operates through information, resources, and sponsorship. ([ResearchGate][7])

### Resilience

[
R_t = (r_{\text{recovery}} r_{\text{emotion regulation}} r_{\text{stress tolerance}})^{1/3}
]

This is best interpreted as **how little your future attempt rate collapses after failure**.

### Focus

[
U_t = u_{\text{deep work}} \cdot (1-u_{\text{switch cost}})
]

where:

* (u_{\text{deep work}}) = fraction of effort done in uninterrupted concentration
* (u_{\text{switch cost}}) = normalized interruption/task-switch burden

This is directly motivated by the attention-residue literature. ([ScienceDirect][9])

### Adaptability

[
A_t = (a_{\text{cognitive}} a_{\text{learning orientation}} a_{\text{update speed}} a_{\text{novelty tolerance}})^{1/4}
]

This is the cleanest way to operationalize adaptability from the literature:

* cognitive ability matters
* learning orientation matters
* adaptive performance is multidimensional
* measurement is noisy, so keep the construct broad but structured ([Paris-Lodron-University Salzburg][10])

Putting it together:

[
P(\text{success by }n)
======================

1-\prod_{t=1}^{n}
\left[
1-
\underbrace{(C_tR_t)}*{\text{attempt actually happens}}
\cdot
\underbrace{
\sigma!\Big(
\operatorname{logit}(p_0)
+\beta_S S_t
+\beta_G G_t
+\beta_T T_t
+\beta_E E_t
+\beta_N N_t
+\beta_U U_t
+\beta*{SN}S_tN_t
+\beta_{GT}G_tT_t
\Big)
}_{\text{success chance of that attempt}}
\right]
]

with dynamics

[
S_{t+1}
=======

S_t+\eta_S C_tU_tF_tA_tE_t(1-S_t)-\lambda_S(1-U_t)S_t
]

[
G_{t+1}
=======

G_t+\eta_G F_tA_t(1-G_t)
]

That is the cleanest “all factors included” version I can defend from the literature.

## 7) What we know vs. what we do not know

What we know pretty well:

* repeated attempts matter mathematically
* practice improves performance, but not enough to explain everything
* consistency/perseverance matters, though it overlaps with conscientiousness
* feedback is conditional, not uniformly good
* specific goals and planning help
* timing and opportunity conditions matter a lot
* resources, network access, focus, and adaptability all materially change outcomes ([PubMed][1])

What we do **not** know:

* there is no single universal coefficient set (\beta) that works for jobs, dating, startups, YouTube, exams, and sales
* objective and subjective measures of adaptation can disagree
* learning curves differ a lot person-to-person
* some constructs are messy and overlapping, especially grit, resilience, and adaptability
* external luck/noise is still real, and this model does not erase it ([Paris-Lodron-University Salzburg][10])

So this formula should be treated as a **research-informed synthesis**, not a proven law of nature.

## 8) The sharpest practical insight

The model says success rises fastest when you do **all four**:

* keep attempts happening: (C, R)
* raise per-attempt quality: (S, G, U, E, N, T)
* improve faster between attempts: (F, A)
* recognize that opportunity conditions can amplify or mute everything: (T)

In plain English:

**show up, learn, adapt, focus, aim well, use resources and relationships intelligently, and keep taking shots while the market window is open.**

That’s annoyingly elegant 😄
